Frequently Asked Questions

A reverse osmosis system is a water filtration process that removes contaminants from water by using pressure to force water molecules through a semipermeable membrane. This effectively separates impurities, including dissolved salts, bacteria, and other harmful substances, providing clean drinking water.

Filters in a reverse osmosis system typically need to be replaced every 6 to 12 months, depending on the water quality and usage. The reverse osmosis membrane usually lasts 2 to 3 years. Always refer to the manufacturer's guidelines for specific replacement schedules.

Yes, reverse osmosis water is safe to drink. The process effectively removes contaminants such as bacteria, viruses, heavy metals, and chemicals, producing high-quality, clean drinking water. However, because it also removes minerals, some people prefer to add mineral additives or opt for systems that remineralize the water.

Yes, reverse osmosis systems are highly effective at removing fluoride from water. The semipermeable membrane used in the process can filter out up to 95% of fluoride along with other contaminants.

Reverse osmosis systems can waste some water, typically about 3 to 5 gallons for every gallon of filtered water produced. However, many modern systems are designed to be more efficient, minimizing water waste. Installing a permeate pump or choosing a system with a high-efficiency waste ratio can help reduce water usage.
